# Inspiring Functions Stories

Short 12-panel graphic novel story ideas exploring the history, development, and real-world applications of mathematical functions. Each story is designed to inspire IB Diploma Programme high school students by humanizing the mathematicians behind the notation and showing how functions shape the modern world.

## 1. "The Language of Change: René Descartes"

- **Short Description:** In 17th century France, René Descartes lay ill in bed watching a fly crawl across his ceiling and wondered how to describe its position with numbers. That daydream became the Cartesian coordinate system, the bridge between algebra and geometry.
- **Inspiration:** Shows how curiosity about ordinary things can unlock entirely new branches of mathematics, and how a single idea can change how humans see the world.

## 2. "The Notation Wars: Leibniz vs. Newton"

- **Short Description:** Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in Germany and Isaac Newton in England independently invented calculus, triggering a bitter international rivalry over who got there first and whose notation would survive. Leibniz's $dy/dx$ won the day — and we still use it.
- **Inspiration:** Demonstrates how good notation is as important as good ideas, and how collaboration across borders ultimately advances science more than competition.

## 3. "The Symbol That Stuck: Leonhard Euler"

- **Short Description:** Swiss mathematician Leonhard Euler, working through blindness in his later years, introduced the notation $f(x)$ that every student uses today. He dictated papers to his sons and kept producing mathematics at a pace nobody has matched since.
- **Inspiration:** Proves that disability does not define ability, and that clear communication can be as revolutionary as discovery itself.

## 4. "The First Programmer: Ada Lovelace"

- **Short Description:** In Victorian London, Ada Lovelace looked at Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine and saw something nobody else did — a machine that could compute any function, not just numbers. She wrote the first algorithm, a century before computers existed.
- **Inspiration:** Shows how imagination can leap ahead of technology, and how women contributed foundational ideas to computing long before the field had a name.

## 5. "The Secret Codebreaker: Alan Turing"

- **Short Description:** During World War II, Alan Turing used mathematical functions and logic to crack the Nazi Enigma code, helping shorten the war by years. His theoretical "Turing machine" became the blueprint for every computer that followed.
- **Inspiration:** Demonstrates that pure mathematics can save lives, and honors a scientist whose brilliance was repaid with persecution — a reminder to protect who we celebrate.

## 6. "Mapping the Unknown: Katherine Johnson"

- **Short Description:** African American mathematician Katherine Johnson used functions and orbital equations to calculate trajectories for NASA's first crewed spaceflights. John Glenn refused to fly until "the girl" checked the computer's numbers by hand.
- **Inspiration:** Shows how trust in mathematics — and in the mathematicians who do it — can literally carry humans to the stars, despite systemic barriers of race and gender.

## 7. "The Shape of Nature: Benoit Mandelbrot"

- **Short Description:** Polish-French mathematician Benoit Mandelbrot asked a strange question — "How long is the coastline of Britain?" — and discovered that the answer depends on how closely you look. His fractal functions revealed hidden patterns in clouds, mountains, and stock markets.
- **Inspiration:** Encourages students to ask questions that seem too simple and to trust that beautiful mathematics can emerge from everyday puzzles.

## 8. "The Equation of Hope: Emmy Noether"

- **Short Description:** German mathematician Emmy Noether, barred from official university positions because she was a woman, quietly developed theorems that became the mathematical foundation of modern physics. Einstein called her the most important woman in the history of mathematics.
- **Inspiration:** Celebrates persistence against discrimination and shows how abstract functions can describe the deepest laws of the physical universe.

## 9. "From Waves to Music: Joseph Fourier"

- **Short Description:** French mathematician Joseph Fourier proposed that any function — no matter how complex — could be built from simple sine and cosine waves. At first, his peers refused to believe it. Today, Fourier's idea powers MP3s, JPEGs, and medical imaging.
- **Inspiration:** Shows how ideas ahead of their time eventually reshape daily life, and how functions you'll meet in class are the same ones streaming music to your phone.

## 10. "The Pandemic Modeler: Ronald Ross"

- **Short Description:** British doctor Ronald Ross used mathematical functions to model how malaria spreads between humans and mosquitoes, proving that reducing mosquito populations could stop the disease. His equations became the foundation of modern epidemiology.
- **Inspiration:** Demonstrates how functions are not just classroom exercises — they save millions of lives by predicting and preventing disease outbreaks.

## 11. "The Invisible Architect: Maryam Mirzakhani"

- **Short Description:** Iranian mathematician Maryam Mirzakhani became the first woman to win the Fields Medal for her work on the geometry of curved surfaces. She described her research as "the joy of discovery" and filled her daughter's playroom with giant sheets of mathematical doodles.
- **Inspiration:** Shows that mathematical creativity looks like art, that women from every country belong at the top of the field, and that joy is a legitimate reason to do mathematics.

## 12. "The Climate Function: Syukuro Manabe"

- **Short Description:** Japanese-American climate scientist Syukuro Manabe built the first mathematical functions that modeled Earth's atmosphere, predicting global warming decades before it was politically accepted. He received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2021.
- **Inspiration:** Shows how patient, careful modeling can warn humanity about planetary-scale problems, and how functions in a textbook today may guide tomorrow's most urgent decisions.

## 13. "The Infinity Dreamer: Srinivasa Ramanujan"

- **Short Description:** A self-taught clerk in colonial India, Srinivasa Ramanujan filled notebooks with astonishing formulas he said came to him from a goddess in dreams. His partnership with G.H. Hardy at Cambridge produced function identities mathematicians are still decoding today.
- **Inspiration:** Celebrates raw mathematical talent from unexpected places and reminds students that mathematical intuition is real — and worth trusting.

## 14. "The Network Weaver: Claude Shannon"

- **Short Description:** American engineer Claude Shannon combined Boolean functions with electrical circuits in his master's thesis, creating the mathematical foundation of the digital age. In his spare time he rode a unicycle while juggling down the halls of Bell Labs.
- **Inspiration:** Shows that playfulness and rigor belong together, and that one clever application of functions can launch an entire technological era.

## 15. "Are We Alone? Frank Drake"

- **Short Description:** In 1961, American astronomer Frank Drake sat down at a chalkboard at the Green Bank Observatory and wrote a single equation with seven variables — the rate of star formation, the fraction with planets, the fraction that could host life, and so on — multiplied together to estimate the number of communicating civilizations in our galaxy. The Drake Equation turned one of humanity's oldest questions into a function we could actually reason about.
- **Inspiration:** Shows how a well-chosen function can transform a seemingly unanswerable philosophical question into a scientific investigation, and how each generation's new data (exoplanets, astrobiology, SETI) updates the inputs and sharpens the answer.

## 16. "Functions for the Future: Karen Uhlenbeck"

- **Short Description:** American mathematician Karen Uhlenbeck, the first woman to win the Abel Prize, used functions and geometry to understand soap bubbles, bridging pure mathematics and theoretical physics. She spent decades mentoring young women in math.
- **Inspiration:** Demonstrates that mentorship multiplies mathematical progress, and that every student sitting in a functions class today is part of a growing, more inclusive mathematical community.
